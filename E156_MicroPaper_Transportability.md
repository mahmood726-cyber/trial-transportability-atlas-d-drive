## Abstract
This study introduces the **Transportability Index (TI)** and **Predicted Evidence Yield (PEY)**, a mathematical framework for quantifying the "clinical distance" between trial origins and global target regions. Using a multi-source synthesis of AACT (n=3,600+), IHME, WHO, and World Bank datasets, we analyze regional readiness across three cardiovascular classes (HFrEF, SGLT2i, GLP-1a).

## 1. The Transportability Index (TI)
The TI measures the structural match between a trial's origin (e.g., North America) and a target region on a scale of 0.0 to 1.0. 
*   **Africa (TI ~0.43)**: Facing a critical workforce bottleneck (0.74 physicians per 1k).
*   **Asia (TI ~0.66)**: Converging on middle-income parity with South America.
*   **South America (TI ~0.83)**: Highest regional workforce density in the trial cohort.

## 2. Predicted Evidence Yield (PEY)
The PEY discounts local disease burden (DALYs) by the Transportability Index to predict actual translational success.
*   **Asia as the Priority**: Despite lower structural readiness than South America, Asia's massive burden (>100M DALYs) results in a PEY ~5.5x higher than any other region.
*   **The African Delivery Gap**: High disease burden in Africa fails to translate into high yield due to low transportability scores.

## 3. Strategic Policy Simulations
Simulations reveal that for Africa, **Workforce-First** interventions (doubling physician density) provide 3x more transportability lift than general economic growth (GDP). In contrast, Asia is now most sensitive to **Economic Uplift** as the final path to North American parity.

## 4. Conclusion
Global health equity is limited by "clinical distance." Future cardiovascular interventions must prioritize workforce hardening in Africa and economic access in Asia to break the divergence trap and successfully transport life-saving clinical evidence.
